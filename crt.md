## Slide 1: Title Slide


**What it says:**
"Clarification on Public Placement Claims" — presented by B.Tech IV Year II Semester (First Batch), School of Technology, Apollo University.



This is the opening slide. It tells everyone that ~125 students from Apollo University's very first B.Tech batch are here to talk about placement promises that don't match reality. The presentation is being given to the "Speak Up Apollo" knowledge team on April 15, 2026.


**Why it matters:**
It sets a serious, professional tone. This isn't a complaint — it's a formal clarification backed by data.

## Slide 2: Who We Are & Why We're Here


**What it says:**
Two sections — "Our Profile" and "Timeline of Concerns."


- **Our Profile:** 125 students, School of Technology, course completed April 14, graduation April 17, and importantly — ZERO alumni before us to guide us.

- **Timeline:**
  - October 2025: Placement drives were promised to begin
  - December 18, 2025: Students submitted a formal written request
  - January–April 2026: Very little structured placement activity happened
  - April 15, 2026: This meeting with Speak Up



We are the first-ever batch of this university. There's nobody who graduated before us to tell us what to expect. We were told placements would start in October 2025, but almost nothing happened. We wrote a formal letter in December asking for clarity, and four months later, we're still waiting — which is why we're here today.


**Why it matters:**
Being the first batch means the university had a special responsibility to get things right. Instead, students were left in the dark for months.

**What it says:**
Three formal requests were made:


1. **Official Written Circular** — A formal, signed document about the current placement status (not just verbal promises).
2. **Company-Wise Clarity** — Which companies visited, how many students were selected, and when offer letters would come.
3. **Placement Roadmap** — What drives are coming next, which companies are in the pipeline, and a clear timeline.


**Status (4 months later):** ❌ No official circular. ❌ No roadmap. ❌ Only verbal, informal communication.



Four months ago, we politely asked the university three simple things: tell us in writing what's happening, give us company-by-company details, and share a plan for what's next. As of today, we've received NONE of these. Everything has been just "talk" with nothing on paper.


**Why it matters:**
Written documents create accountability. Verbal promises can be denied later. The university's refusal to put anything in writing is itself a red flag.

## Slide 5: "39 Offers" — Breaking Down the Numbers


**What it says:**
The university claims 39 placement offers. Here's the real breakdown:


| Category | Details | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| **Infosys (Off-Campus)** | ~11 students, government-organized drive, no university role, actual pay ₹3.25 LPA (not ₹7 LPA as claimed) | NOT a university placement |
| **DB4 Technologies** | ~11 students, serious credibility concerns, employee warnings documented | Quality concerns |
| **Training Institutes** | QSpiders, Besant Technologies — these are training programs, not employment offers | NOT employment |
| **Other Companies** | 1-2 students each, no structured recruitment | Scattered |


**Bottom line:** Actual university-facilitated placements = **12-16 students (≈10-13%)** out of 125.



The university says "39 offers!" — but let's look closely. About 11 came from Infosys through a government drive (the university had nothing to do with it). Another 11 came from DB4 Technologies, a company whose own employees warned students not to join. Some "offers" were from training institutes like QSpiders and Besant Technologies — these are training programs, not actual jobs. When you remove all the inflated numbers, only about 12-16 students actually got real placements through the university.


**Why it matters:**
Counting off-campus drives and training institutes as "placements" is dishonest. It inflates numbers to look good on paper while students remain unemployed.

## Slide 6: Case Study — Infosys


**What it says:**


| Claimed | Reality |
|---|---|
| Campus placement | Government of AP organized it |
| ₹7 LPA package | Actual package: ₹3.25 LPA |
| University facilitated | University had NO role — students applied independently via government portal |


~11 students got placed through this drive.


**Key Question:** Should government-organized off-campus drives — where the university had no active role — count as campus placements?



The university claims Infosys placements as their achievement. But here's the truth: the Government of Andhra Pradesh organized this drive at an Infosys office (not on campus). Students applied on their own through a government website. The university did nothing except maybe share the information. Also, the actual salary is ₹3.25 LPA, but the university told people it was ₹7 LPA — more than double the real number.


**Why it matters:**
This is the clearest example of taking credit for something you didn't do, and inflating the salary figure on top of that.

## Slide 7: Case Study — DB4 Technologies


**What it says:**
DB4 Technologies was presented as a campus placement. ~11 students were placed. But there are serious red flags:


1. **Unusual Marketing Requests:** Students were asked to record appreciation/testimonial videos BEFORE even joining the company — for the company's marketing purposes.
2. **Employee Warnings (Audio Evidence):** Existing DB4 employees directly contacted students saying "Do not join" — citing concerns about work culture and company stability.


**Core Principle:** Universities must verify companies before inviting them to campus. Students trust that the placement cell has vetted these companies.



This was the only real on-campus drive of any size. But the company asked students to make promotional videos before they even started working — that's suspicious. Even worse, people already working at DB4 secretly warned our students not to join, saying the company has serious problems. We have audio recordings as proof. The university should have checked this company's credibility before bringing them to campus.


**Why it matters:**
If the one major on-campus recruiter turns out to be questionable, it shows the placement cell isn't doing basic due diligence. Students are being sent to potentially harmful workplaces.

## Slide 8: Training Institutes ≠ Placements


**What it says:**
QSpiders and Besant Technologies were counted as "placements." But these are training institutes, not employers:


- These are training programs, not job offers
- No jobs are guaranteed after completion
- Documented proof: An HR email from Besant Technologies (April 15, 2026) shows 6 named students completed training courses (SQL, Excel, Python, Django) but received **ZERO placements** afterward.



Imagine you're told "You got placed!" but then you find out it's just a training program, not an actual job. And after finishing the training, there's no job waiting for you. That's exactly what happened. QSpiders and Besant Technologies are training centers, not companies that hire you. Six students are named specifically — they finished the full training and got nothing. Counting this as "placement" is like counting a gym membership as an athletic achievement.


**Why it matters:**
This is the most misleading category. Training programs are not employment. Including these in placement numbers is fundamentally dishonest.

## Slide 9: Low-Quality Drives


**What it says:**
B.Tech Computer Science engineers were directed to:


- **BPO/Call Centers:** Customer service, telecalling, ₹10,000-15,000/month, no technical work
- **Sales & Marketing:** Direct sales, field marketing, commission-based, zero engineering relevance
- **Admin/Clerical:** Data entry, administrative positions, below qualification level


**Also noted:** The university did NOT pay ByteXL (a training partner) their 3rd installment, even though students completed the program.


**Student position:** "We're not being elitist — we're willing to start anywhere in RELEVANT fields. After 4 years of CS engineering + ₹8-10 lakhs in fees: Quality > Quantity."



After studying computer science for four years and paying ₹8-10 lakhs in fees, students were offered jobs like answering phone calls at a call center for ₹10,000-15,000 per month, or going door-to-door for sales. These jobs have nothing to do with engineering. On top of that, the university didn't even pay their own training vendor (ByteXL) — raising questions about where the money goes. The students are clear: they're not demanding fancy jobs, just RELEVANT ones.


**Why it matters:**
Offering irrelevant, low-paying jobs to engineering graduates and counting them as "placements" defeats the entire purpose of a placement cell.

## Slide 10: Financial Accountability — The ₹17,000 Question


**What it says:**


- **₹17,000** was charged per student for CRT (Campus Recruitment Training)
- **95 students** paid this = **₹16,15,000** total collected
- **Promised:** Training, mock interviews, resume building, soft skills ✓ (these were delivered)
- **Not Delivered:** 90% placement rate, 250+ recruiters, proportional outcomes, meaningful industry connections ✗


**Evidence:** Audio recording from December 1, 2025 meeting where these commitments were discussed.



Each student paid ₹17,000 for special placement training — that's over ₹16 lakhs collected from just one batch. The training classes did happen (mock interviews, resume help, etc.). But the OUTCOMES promised alongside that fee — like a 90% placement rate and connections with 250+ companies — never materialized. Students aren't asking for refunds; they're asking: "Where did this money go, and why didn't it deliver results?"


**Why it matters:**
When you collect money with specific promises attached, you need to show what those promises achieved. The gap between promise and delivery is enormous.

## Slide 14: What We're Asking For


**What it says:**
Four clear demands:


1. **Official Clarification** — Verify the 90% and 250+ claims, provide batch-specific data, define what "placement" means, remove/label placeholder data.
2. **Documented Communication** — Official written circular, company-wise details, formal roadmap with timelines, accountability trail.
3. **Accountability Mechanism** — Clear responsibility assignment, CRT fee justification, grievance redressal process, escalation pathway.
4. **Quality Standards** — Criteria for inviting companies, role-education alignment minimums, company credibility vetting, post-offer student protection.



We want four things:
1. **Tell the truth** — Either prove the 90% claim or correct it publicly.
2. **Put it in writing** — Give us official documents, not verbal promises.
3. **Be accountable** — Someone needs to own the outcomes and justify the fees collected.
4. **Set standards** — Stop inviting call centers and training institutes. Only bring companies that are relevant and verified.


These are reasonable, professional asks — not unreasonable demands.


**Why it matters:**
This slide shows the students aren't asking for guarantees or impossible things. They're asking for basic transparency and professionalism.
